The Breaking Point

It was late on a Tuesday night...

I was standing in my bathroom in Surulere.

The light was flickering because NEPA had taken light and my generator was too low on fuel to run the AC.

I was staring at myself in the mirror.

Just staring.

My wrapper — the same wrapper I wore to my traditional wedding just two years before — was hanging on the door hook behind me.

I reached for it.

Tried to tie it the way I used to. The way that made my hips look like something from a Nollywood movie.

But the wrapper just... hung there.

Like a curtain.

Like a used wrapper.

I looked at my reflection and thought: "Adaobi, you don not become just mother. You don not become woman again."

My hand was shaking as I touched my stomach. The skin felt soft. Too soft. Like puff-puff that sat too long in oil.

And lower... lower where I couldn't see but I could FEEL...

It felt empty. Loose. Like a bag of garri with too much air inside.

I started crying.

Not the loud crying.

The silent kind.

The kind where your chest heaves but no sound comes out because you're ashamed that you're even crying about your own body.

I gave everything to carry my baby. And I felt like I lost myself in the process.

Three Weeks Later

I was at my sister's owambe in Ikeja. The music was playing. Buga by Kizz Daniel. Everyone was dancing.

I used to LOVE dancing at owambe.

I used to be the one they called to come and show them how to do the shaku shaku properly.

But that day?

I was sitting at the back table. Wearing a long dress — the longest one I could find. Black. Loose. Hiding everything.

My friend Nkechi came over. She pulled my hand.

"Ada, come and dance na! Why you dey behave like old mama?"

I looked at her. I wanted to tell her. I wanted to say:

"Nkechi, I can't dance because if I dance, I will leak. If I laugh too hard, I will leak. If I jump, everything down there moves like water in a bucket."

But I couldn't say it.

So I smiled. Fake smile.

"I'm just tired," I said. "The baby kept me up."

Nkechi looked at me.

She knew I was lying.

I saw it in her eyes.

That pity look.

That "poor Adaobi, motherhood don destroy her" look.

I went to the toilet. Locked the door. Sat on the floor — yes, the dirty toilet floor — and I put my head in my hands.

I was wearing a pad. A thick pad. Not because I was on my period.

But because I was afraid. Afraid that if I laughed, if I sneezed, if I moved too fast... I would wet myself.

At thirty-two years old.

I sat there on that toilet floor and I thought:

"Adaobi, you have become something you don't recognize. You have become a used wrapper. Wrung out. Stretched. Thrown aside."

I didn't go back to the party.

I went home.

Changed my wrapper.

Put on the biggest boubou I owned.

And I lay on my bed staring at the ceiling until 3 AM.

The Vow — 2:47 AM

That night... at exactly 2:47 AM... I made a vow.

I was lying there. My husband was snoring beside me. My baby was asleep in the next room. And I was awake. Crying silently.

Feeling like my body was a stranger who had stolen my life.

I sat up.

I looked at my phone. The screen was cracked — same crack from when I dropped it three months ago and couldn't afford to fix it because all the money was going to baby things.

And I said out loud — whispered, actually —

"I will find the answer. I will read every book. I will ask every woman. I will crawl under every rock in this Lagos if necessary. I will get my body back. Or I will die trying."

I didn't know how.

I didn't know where.

But I knew I would not spend one more year feeling like a used wrapper.

Three Months Later — Anambra State

I was in my village. Anambra State. For my grandmother's burial.

After the ceremony, I was helping clean the kitchen.

An old woman was sitting in the corner.

Mama Ngozi. She was my grandmother's friend. Sixty-seven years old. A retired midwife who had delivered babies in our village for forty years.

She called me over.

"Adaobi," she said. "Come."

I walked to her. She was stirring something in a small clay pot. The smell was... herbal. Earthy. Like something from the forest mixed with something sweet.

She looked at me.

Those old eyes saw everything.

"You don born," she said. Not a question. A statement.

I nodded.

"And you don lose yourself," she said.

My eyes filled with tears. Just like that. Because she said it. She named it. The thing I couldn't name to my own husband.

She reached into her wrapper. Pulled out a small notebook. Old. Torn at the edges. Pages yellow like agege bread.

"Sit down," she said.

I sat on the wooden stool. She opened the notebook.

Inside were drawings. Leaves. Roots. Seeds. Things I didn't recognize. Each page had notes in her shaky handwriting.

"This one," she said, pointing to a drawing of a leaf, "we call it the 'Woman's Crown' in our language. The village women have used it for generations. This one," she pointed to a root, "this is the 'Dark Finger.' Very powerful. And this one," she touched a drawing of small seeds, "this is what we call the 'Seed of Restoration.' The secret of the mothers."

She looked at me.

"For forty years," she said, "I see women come after baby. Crying. Ashamed. Feeling like their body don leave them. The hospital give them exercise. Kegel this. Pelvic that. But these women? They don't need exercise. They need restoration."

She tapped her notebook.

"The village women," she said, "they know. After baby, they don't run to gym. They don't do exercise like white woman. They sit. They steam. They drink. They wash. With herbs. The same herbs their grandmother use. The same herbs I write in this book."

She leaned close. Her breath smelled of ginger and something else. Something ancient.

"In thirty-one days," she whispered, "the body remember. The body tightens. Not because you force am with exercise. Because you give am what it need to heal."

She closed the book. Put it in my hand.

"Take," she said. "But don't just read. Do."
